\subsection{Materials}
The feedstock used in this study is a digestate originating from a slaughterhouse located in France, hereafter referred to as the primary substrate. For comparative purposes, a digestate from a slaughterhouse located in Algeria was also analyzed and included, albeit not as the main focus, hereafter referred to as the secondary substrate. Both materials are residues from anaerobic digestion supplied by external sources. No further information regarding the feedstock composition, digestion process, or operating conditions of the originating biogas plants was available.

\vspace{0.3em}

The digestate was used in its as-received state for the pyrolysis experiments. After pyrolysis, the resulting biochars were stored in a desiccator to prevent moisture uptake during subsequent storage and handling. A separate drying step using a moisture-analyzing balance (PCE MB 210C, PCE Instruments) was applied to digestate and biochar samples prior to calorific value determination.

The reagents and gasses employed in the activation and sulfurization steps were:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Deionized water ($H_2O$) - Activation medium
    \item Argon, $Ar$ [Purity grade to be confirmed] - Inert gas for purging prior to activation
    \item Iron(II) sulfide, $FeS$ [Supplier and purity to be confirmed] - sulfur precursor
    \item Hydrochloric acid, HCL [Concentration and supplier to be confirmed] - acid for $H_2S$ generation
\end{itemize}

All experiments were carried out at the laboratories of Hochschule für Technik und Wirtschaft Berlin.

\subsection{Biochar Production via Pyrolysis}
\label{biocharproduction}
Biochar was produced from the slaughterhouse digestate by slow pyrolysis in an electrically heated laboratory-scale oven (Hochschule für Technik und Wirtschaft Berlin). The setup consisted of a programmable temperature-controlled oven and a sealable pyrolysis reactor, as shown in Figure %REFERENCE.

\vspace{0.3em}

Prior to each run, the reactor was thoroughly cleaned to ensure consistency between experiments, and the gas outlet pipe was inspected to prevent blockages during operation. Due to the limited quantity of the primary substrate (Slaughterhouse digestate, France), pyrolysis was carried out using ceramic crucibles (Fig. xx) placed inside the reactor rather than by filling the reactor volume directly, as illustrated in Figure [X].

\vspace{0.3em}

For each run, a known mass of digestate (recorded before pyrolysis) was loaded into the crucibles, and an additional crucible containing a defined mass of water was placed alongside the sample crucibles. The water generated water vapor during heating, which helped maintain an oxygen-poor atmosphere within the reactor. 

\vspace{0.3em}

The reactor was first sealed with 6 screws, then placed inside the oven, and heated according to the following programmed conditions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Heating rate : 10 °C min$^{-1}$
    \item Target temperatures : 400 °C, 500 °C, or 600 °C
    \item Residence time at target temperatures: 1 h
\end{itemize}

Three pyrolysis temperatures were investigated to study the influence of pyrolysis temperature on biochar yield and properties, as discussed in \S~\ref{Influence pyrolysis condition}. After the residence time, the oven was switched off and the reactor was allowed to cool down to relative lower temperature before opening, in order to avoid oxidation of the freshly produced biochar by atmospheric oxygen.

Only the solid product (biochar) was collected and quantified in this study. The condensable bio-oil and non-condensable gas fractions were neither quantified nor characterized. The biochar yield Y was calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    Y = \frac{m_{Biochar}}{m_{Digestate}} \times 100 \%
\end{equation}
where $m_{Digestate}$ is the initial mass of digestate loaded into the reactor and $m_{Biochar}$ is the mass of the solid product recovered after pyrolysis. The collected biochars were transferred to plastic containers with name labels on them and stored until further processing in the activation step.



\subsection{\texorpdfstring{H$_2$O}{H2O}-Activation}
To develop the porous structure of the biochar produced in \S~\ref{biocharproduction}, a steam activation step was carried out in a separate setup consisting of a horizontal tube furnace (Carbolite) and a reaction tube placed coaxially inside the furnace, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:3.3.2}. The reaction tube is sealed at both ends by valve-controlled fittings, allowing controlled gas flow during heat-up and full isolation of the reaction volume once the steam phase is initiated.

The amount of water introduced per activation was selected to limit the mass loss of the biochar to below 10\%, in line with the burn-off control objective established in the project work. Although the underlying intent is to limit "carbon" loss, the experimentally accessible quantity in this work is the total mass loss of the biochar before and after activation. The two are equivalent only under the assumption that the steam-carbon reaction :
\begin{equation}
    C \, + H_2O \rightarrow CO \, + H_2
\end{equation}
is the sole mass-removing process. In practice, residual devolatilization of hydrogen- and oxygen-containing functional groups may also contribute to the measured mass loss.

\vspace{0.3em}

A stoichiometric calculation based on this reaction and a representative biochar carbon content of approximately 60\% [Reference] indicates that, at a uniform tube temperature of 700 °C, only about 0.09 g of water ($\approx$ 0.09 mL at STP) would theoretically be required to gasify 0.06 g of carbon. In practice, however, the temperature is not uniform across the entire tube volume; the central hot zone reaches the set point while the tube extremities remain considerably cooler, so that a non-negligible fraction of the injected water condenses on the cooler tube walls and is not available for reaction with the biochar. 

\vspace{0.3em}

To verify that the 10\% mass-loss target could be met under these non-ideal conditions, a comparative trial was carried out with two water doses applied to 1 g biochar samples from each pyrolysis temperature : 0.1 mL (close to the theoretical minimum) and 0.5 mL (a fivefold excess intended to compensate for steam losses to condensation). Both doses kept the measured mass loss well below the 10\% threshold, and the resulting losses were essentially identical between the two doses for the 500 °C and 600 °C biochars (see. [X]). This indicates that under the closed-tube conditions used in this work, water is not the limiting factor. 

\vspace{0.3em}

On this basis, the 0.5 mL of water per 1 g of biochar dose was adopted as the standard activation condition. All activated biochar samples were used thereafter for the elemental and surface analyzes described in \S~\ref{sec: Analyticalmethods} were produced under this condition. The 0.1 mL dose was retained only as a comparative reference for the activation study.

\subsubsection*{Activation procedure}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inert purging and heat-up} \newline
    Argon was introduced from the upstream end (or left side of Figure \ref{fig:3.3.2}) of the tube and allowed to exit through the other end of the tube valve. The argon flow was kept deliberately low to maintain an inert atmosphere without blowing off the biochar. Simultaneously, the furnace was switched on and programmed to reach a set point of 700 °C at a heating rate of 10 K min$^{-1}$.
    \item \textbf{Transition to steam atmosphere} \newline
    Argon pushing was continued until the downstream end (or right side of Figure \ref{fig:3.3.2}) of the tube reached a temperature above 100 °C. This ensures that injected liquid water would flash to vapor rather than condense at the injection point. At this point, before injection, the argon flow was stopped and both valves of the tube were closed.
    \item \textbf{Water injection and sealed activation} \newline
    A single dose of 0.5 mL of deionized water was manually injected through the downstream valve using a syringe. The downstream valve was closed immediately after injection, isolating the reaction tube from the surrounding atmosphere. Under these closed-tube conditions, the injected water flashed to steam in the hot central region and reacted with the biochar over the activation period. The 1 h activation period was timed from the moment of water injection. Some condensation of steam in the cooler tube extremities is expected. Consequently, not all of the injected water vapor is assumed to participate in the steam-carbon reaction.
    \item \textbf{Cool-down} \newline
    After the 1 h hold, the furnace was switched off and the system was allowed to cool to ambient temperature overnight.

    After cooling, the activated biochar was recovered from the reactor tube and weighed. The activation mass loss was calculated on a wet basis as:
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta m_{active,wet}=\frac{m_{before}-m_{after,wet}}{m_{before}} \times 100\%
    \end{equation}
    Because water was introduced during activation by spraying, this initial post-activation mass still contained moisture that had to be separated from the actual mass change caused by activation. Residual moisture was determined gravimetrically using a halogen moisture analyser (PCE-MA series, PCE Instruments GmbH, Germany) operating on the loss-on-drying (LOD) principle. The instrument heats the sample by means of halogen lamps and calculates moisture content from the mass difference before and after drying; a drying temperature of 190 °C was used. As the analyser required a minimum sample mass to produce a stable reading, two inert metal rings (combined mass $m_{Nutrings}$=20.74 g) were placed in the sample pan together with each biochar sample to meet this threshold. The rings were first dried so they contributed no moisture of their own, meaning the entire water mass detected during drying originated from the biochar.
    
    Because the analyzer reports moisture as a fraction of the total mass in the chamber, the reported value $w_{dryer}$ refers to the combined mass of biochar and metal rings rather than to the biochar alone. To get the true biochar moisture content:
    \begin{equation}
        w_{bio}=\frac{w_{dryer}\times(m_{after,wet}+m_{Nutrings})}{m_{after,wet}}
    \end{equation}
where $m_{after,wet}$ is the post-activation mass of the biochar prior to drying. After this  the value of $m_{after,dry}$ could be known thus also the total mass loss both from activation and also from the moisture which can be described as :
\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_{loss}=\frac{m_{before}-m_{after,dry}}{m_{before}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta m_{loss}=\frac{m_{before}-(m_{after,wet}(1-w_{dryer}))}{m_{before}} \times 100\%
    \end{equation}
    where $m_{before}$ is the mass of biochar loaded into the tube and $m_{after,dry}$ is the post-activation mass after drying. The activated biochars were stored in a glass container until further use in the sulphurization step.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sulfur Loading}
\setresponsible{Regen Wijaya}

The sulfur loading of the activated biochar was conducted using simple laboratory-scale setup that resembles the principle process of biogas desulfurization. The setup consists of magnetic stirrer, reaction flask equipped with dropping funnel, glass tubes and connector tube (Figure \ref{fig:Schwsetup}), all of which are placed in an enclosed chamber equipped with a suction function.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{Abbildungen/Schwefelbeladung Setup .jpg}
  \caption{The setup to load sulfur on the surface of biochar}
  \label{fig:Schwsetup}
\end{figure}

\newpage

Firstly, the activated biochars were placed inside glass tubes with both ends open and plugged at both ends with cotton wool to keep the biochars stay in place while allowing the gaseous flow of the hydrogen sulfide passing through the biochars without causing blockage. Each tube was connected with Y-connector, which was linked to an air source and a reaction flask. 

\vspace{0.3em}

In the reaction flask, pulverized iron sulfide was placed and reacted gradually with droplets of hydrochloric acid from the funnel until the formation and release of hydrogen sulfide gas occurred, which can be characterized by the presence of continuous bubbling in the solution. The generated gaseous hydrogen sulfide was then directed to Y-connector, where the $H_2S$ stream was introduced to air stream, resulting to partial oxidation of $H_2S$ to elemental sulfur and water. The resulting gaseous mixture is then led through the activated biochar in each glass tube. This process is allowed to continue for a sufficiently long time so that as much sulfur as possible can be absorbed by the charcoal. 

\vspace{0.3em}

To quantitatively evaluate the extent of sulfur loading onto biochar, the activated biochars are weighed before and after the adsorption process. The weight gain provides an accurate assessment of the sulfur adsorption capacity. Additionally, microscopic characterization techniques, such as scanning electron microscopy (SEM), could further elucidate changes in biochar morphology post-adsorption, thereby providing insights into the distribution and structural integration of sulfur on biochar surfaces.

\subsubsection{Calorific Value Determination (Bomb Calorimetry)}
\label{Calorificvaluedetermination}
The higher heating value (HHV) of the raw digestate and of the biochars was determined using an isoperibolic bomb calorimeter (IKA C 200, IKA-Werke GmbH \& Co. KG) following the principle described in \S~\ref{subsubsec:HHV}. The instrument was calibrated at the beginning of each measurement campaign using certified benzoic acid pellets, which served as a reference material for determining the effective heat capacity $\varepsilon$ of the calorimeter system.

Prior to each measurement, samples were dried to remove residual moisture using a halogen moisture analyzer (PCE-MB 210C) Operated at 100°C Until constant mass was reached, with the device evaluating dryness via a switch-off criterion of 10 s without further mass change. The dried samples were transferred to a desiccator and kept there until measurement to prevent the re-uptake of atmospheric moisture. The moisture content displayed by the device was used solely as a process control to confirm that the sample had reached a dry state and not to be reported as an independent result.

For each measurement, approximately 1.0 g of dried sample was placed in the crucible and then inserted into the bomb. The bomb was sealed and pressurized with pure oxygen to ensure complete combustion. Combustion was initiated electrically via an ignition wire in contact with a thread, which was also in contact and buried within the sample. The temperature rise of the surrounding water bath was recorded by the instrument, and the gross calorific value $qV,{gr}$ was calculated automatically by the device according to Equation \ref{equ: qvgr} in \S~\ref{subsubsec:HHV}.

Each Sample was measured in triplicate (n=3). Mean values and standard deviations are reported in Chapter 5. %INI NANTI di REFERENSI kan

\subsubsection{CHNS Elemental Analysis}
The mass fractions of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), nitrogen (N), and sulfur (S) in the raw digestate, pyrolyzed biochars, activated biochars, and sulfur-loaded biochars were determined by combustion-based CHNS elemental analysis, based on the principle described in \S~ 2.5. The analyses were carried out as a contracted service by the Technische Universität Berlin, as the corresponding instrumentation was not available at HTW Berlin.

Sample preparation on the HTW Berlin side consisted of drying each sample and storing them in sealed containers.
